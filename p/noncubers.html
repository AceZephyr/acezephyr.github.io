For Non-cubers:

Hello! I am a speedcuber. I solve Rubik's Cubes fast.

A lot of you may have a lot of misconceptions about us and the hobby of speedcubing.

Here, I will clear up misconceptions, stereotypes, and other misinformation about us.

Also, if you are writing a news article about cubing, this will show you how not to write one that makes us cringe.

Some Misconceptions:

First of all, no, just because you can solve a Rubik's cube doesn't mean you are amazing at math and science.

It's more about recognizing patterns and reacting to them in the best way.


Second, no, the goal of solving a Rubik's cube is not to match all six sides. Even though that's the solved state, it's a horrible way to think about actually solving it.

If you were to take apart a Rubik's Cube, you'd see that it's made up of pieces, not individual stickers.

The twelve edge pieces will always have exactly two stickers each, and those stickers won't change.

The eight corner pieces will always have exactly three stickers each, and those stickers won't change.

Finally, the centers are all attached to each other, and they don't move relative to each other.

Each piece has exactly one place it can be on the final cube and exactly one orientation it can be in.

The challenge comes in moving those pieces to their solved position and orientation without disturbing the other pieces that have already been solved as quickly as possible.

We've come up with a lot of methods that let us do this fast, and we get better at using those methods to get faster at solving the cube.

Most people use one called CFOP. Most of the resources on my website are about CFOP. as that is also the method I use.

In fact, the beginner's method under "How to solve a Rubik's Cube" is similar to CFOP, but broken down to be as easy as possible rather than as fast as possible.

In fact, I encourage you to learn how to solve the cube, even if you don't plan on becoming a speedcuber.


Finally, it's spelled Rubik's, not Rubix, Rubiks, Rubicks, Rubics, or any other misspelling of the name of the Hungarian inventor of the cube, Ern≈ë Rubik.

Rubik's Cube was a cube made by a person whose last name is Rubik. That is why it is called Rubik's Cube. It is his cube. He made it.

For the news:

If you're writing about a new Rubik's Cube World Record, it's also a good idea to understand the basics as to how a Rubik's Cube competition works.

A group of competetors are called up to the solving area to have their cubes scrambled.

They each perform five solves. They use a timer with two pads that begins after one of their hands is off its pad and ends when both pads are pressed again.

Their average time for those five solves is calculated by ignoring the fastest and slowest solves and taking the mean of the remaining three.

For each competetor's first solve, they all get the same scramble.

For each competetor's second solve, they all get the same scramble, which is different from the first one. This repeats for all five solves.

Before the solve, the comeptetor has fifteen seconds they are allowed to look at the puzzle for before starting the timer. This is called 'inspection'.
